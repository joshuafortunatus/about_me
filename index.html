Platform Migration

Looker → Hex Migration

The problem

  • Poor legacy implementation — Looker had become a "kitchen sink," trying to be everything for everyone for every use case, instead of focusing on who used the data most and what they were actually trying to answer.
  • Data fidelity & definitions — inconsistent metric definitions across dashboards eroded trust in the numbers.
  • Blocker to exploratory analysis — even simple questions were too slow to answer, throttling stakeholders who already had deep domain knowledge from self-serving.

The approach

  • Treated dbt and BI as a single ecosystem, where each layer informs the other, instead of retrofitting BI on top of a legacy model.
  • Centralized metric definitions in one place with a "narrow waist" approach, so every downstream tool inherits the same source of truth.
  • Layered AI-assisted answers on top of that narrow waist — with the right context, an agent can resolve low-to-medium complexity questions for stakeholders who already have deep domain knowledge, closing the gap on simple-but-slow asks.

How

  • Built context guides so similar questions converge on increasingly deterministic answers
  • Version-controlled everything through git
  • Ran hands-on training as adoption rolled out

Outcome

Overwhelming support for Hex during the trial period, with excitement growing as adoption broadened — more trust in the data, and more collaboration across teams.

 dbt Optimization

$50K Annual Compute Savings

The problem

  • Nightly dbt runtime had more than doubled over the course of a year, quietly eating into the morning reporting window.
  • Runs were sometimes still finishing when East Coast employees logged on, so they started their day looking at stale data.

The approach

  • Identified the long-running models actually driving the runtime growth.
  • Triaged each one: optimize it for real usage, or deprecate it if it wasn't earning its runtime.

How

  • Queried usage logs to find models that were active in the DAG but never actually surfaced to stakeholders in the BI layer, and removed them.
  • Materialized the largest remaining models as incremental instead of full-refresh.
  • Rebuilt remaining models around actual use cases and their primary stakeholders.
  • Right-sized warehouse compute for the models that still needed it.

Outcome

  • Cut nightly runtime by 40%.
  • Cut compute costs by $50K annually.
  • Established more rigorous modeling guidelines to keep the DAG from bloating again.
 Fixed Effects

Ad Effectiveness & Elasticity

The problem

  • Dispensaries that run sponsored placements aren't like those that don't — bigger, faster-growing menus adopt ads first, so any ads-vs-no-ads comparison is confounded from the start.
  • Before/after comparisons are equally polluted: the whole market moves with holidays, promos, and seasonality, so a dispensary's "lift" may just be everyone's good day.
  • "Does turning ads on matter?" and "does spending more matter?" are different questions that a single model can't answer cleanly.

The approach

  • Two-way fixed-effects panel: every dispensary is compared only to itself (its own ads-on vs. ads-off days), with each calendar day's market-wide baseline estimated and subtracted out.
  • Two complementary models: a binary on/off comparison for whether active placements matter, and a log-log impression-elasticity model for whether scaling volume matters — with diminishing returns built into the functional form.
  • Effects estimated overall and by customer tier, since a pooled average can hide who actually benefits.

How

  • Daily dispensary-level panel of sales and ads activity; standard errors clustered by dispensary; tier effects via interaction terms.
  • Robustness through re-specification: first-differences models at both daily and weekly grain independently reproduce a significant effect in the same range, so the finding isn't an artifact of one modeling choice.
  • Causality bounded honestly: the design removes fixed dispensary traits and market-wide shocks, but can't rule out simultaneous changes (a promo launched alongside ads) — flagged explicitly, with a user-level A/B test named as the definitive follow-up.

Outcome

  • Dispensaries see significantly more GMV and more orders on days sponsored placements are active, with essentially no change in basket size — ads convert more browsers into buyers rather than making buyers spend more.
  • More impressions drive more revenue with diminishing returns, and the effect is significant in every tier — including the smallest dispensaries, where the blunt on/off signal alone was too noisy to detect.
  • Larger dispensaries show the smallest incremental returns to scaling impressions, consistent with a higher baseline that's harder to move.
 Difference-in-Differences

Pay by Bank AOV Lift

The problem

  • Industry-wide price compression has AOV falling for years — any dollar-denominated lift metric shrinks over time even if PBB's relative advantage is constant.
  • Adopters differ from non-adopters (higher baselines, staggered adoption timing), so naive before/after or adopter-vs-rest comparisons are confounded.
  • "Order frequency" is invisible to standard panels: measuring only the months a customer shops can't distinguish "shops more often" from "shops the same, in fewer months."

The approach

  • Callaway–Sant'Anna staggered difference-in-differences: each adopter cohort vs. customers who hadn't adopted yet, reported in percentages only.
  • Dollar outcomes estimated in logs, so the parallel-trends assumption is about rates of change, not dollars — a proportional market decline cancels out instead of masquerading as a shrinking effect.
  • Effects averaged over a fixed two-year post-adoption window with bootstrap confidence intervals; the adoption month itself excluded (mechanically contains the first PBB order).

How

  • Monthly customer-level panel; tens of thousands of adopters and matched-scale never-adopted controls, stratified and inverse-probability weighted.
  • Diagnostics as gates, not formalities: pre-trend placebos, anticipation correction, data-quality audits, and a cohort-support check justifying the analysis window.
  • The frequency estimate was stress-tested with placebo adoptions — never-adopters given fake adoption dates reproduced the raw estimate almost exactly with zero treatment, exposing it as a customer-lifecycle artifact rather than a PBB effect.

Outcome

  • Statistically significant lift in order value, and a larger lift in spend within the months customers shop — both robust to price compression by construction.
  • No detectable change in order frequency — the same order count consolidates into fewer, denser shopping months.
  • Net: total customer spend rises modestly (on the order of the basket-size effect); the larger per-shopping-month figure describes concentration, not new dollars.
