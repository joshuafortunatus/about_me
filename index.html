Difference-in-Differences

Pay by Bank AOV Lift

Problem

  • Industry-wide price compression had AOV falling for years — any dollar-denominated lift metric shrank over time even if PBB's relative advantage stayed constant
  • Adopters differed from non-adopters (higher baselines, staggered adoption timing), so naive before/after or adopter-vs-rest comparisons were confounded
  • "Order frequency" was invisible to standard panels: measuring only the months a customer shopped couldn't distinguish "shopped more often" from "shopped the same, in fewer months"

Approach

  • Callaway–Sant'Anna staggered difference-in-differences: each adopter cohort vs. customers who hadn't adopted yet, reported in percentages only
  • Dollar outcomes were estimated in logs, so the parallel-trends assumption was about rates of change, not dollars — a proportional market decline canceled out instead of masquerading as a shrinking effect
  • Effects were averaged over a fixed two-year post-adoption window with bootstrap confidence intervals; the adoption month itself was excluded (it mechanically contained the first PBB order)

Execution

  • Monthly customer-level panel; tens of thousands of adopters and matched-scale never-adopted controls, stratified and inverse-probability weighted
  • Diagnostics as gates, not formalities: pre-trend placebos, anticipation correction, data-quality audits, and a cohort-support check justifying the analysis window
  • The frequency estimate was stress-tested with placebo adoptions — never-adopters given fake adoption dates reproduced the raw estimate almost exactly with zero treatment, exposing it as a customer-lifecycle artifact rather than a PBB effect

Outcome

  • Statistically significant lift in order value, and a larger lift in spend within the months customers shopped — both robust to price compression by construction
  • No detectable change in order frequency — the same order count consolidated into fewer, denser shopping months
  • Net: total customer spend rose modestly (on the order of the basket-size effect); the larger per-shopping-month figure described concentration, not new dollars
 Platform Migration

Looker → Hex Migration

Problem

  • Poor legacy implementation — Looker had become a "kitchen sink," trying to be everything for everyone for every use case, instead of focusing on who used the data most and what they were actually trying to answer
  • Data fidelity & definitions — inconsistent metric definitions across dashboards eroded trust in the numbers
  • Blocker to exploratory analysis — even simple questions were too slow to answer, throttling stakeholders who already had deep domain knowledge from self-serving

Approach

  • Treated dbt and BI as a single ecosystem, where each layer informs the other, instead of retrofitting BI on top of a legacy model
  • Centralized metric definitions in one place with a "narrow waist" approach, so every downstream tool inherits the same source of truth
  • Layered AI-assisted answers on top of that narrow waist — with the right context, an agent can resolve low-to-medium complexity questions for stakeholders who already have deep domain knowledge, closing the gap on simple-but-slow asks

Execution

  • Built context guides so similar questions converge on increasingly deterministic answers
  • Version-controlled everything through git
  • Ran hands-on training as adoption rolled out

Outcome

Overwhelming support for Hex during the trial period, with excitement growing as adoption broadened — more trust in the data, and more collaboration across teams.

 Fixed Effects

Ad Effectiveness & Elasticity

Problem

  • Dispensaries that ran sponsored placements weren't like those that didn't — bigger, faster-growing menus adopted ads first, so any ads-vs-no-ads comparison was confounded from the start
  • Before/after comparisons were equally polluted: the whole market moved with holidays, promos, and seasonality, so a dispensary's "lift" was often just everyone's good day
  • "Did turning ads on matter?" and "did spending more matter?" were different questions that a single model couldn't answer cleanly

Approach

  • Two-way fixed-effects panel: every dispensary was compared only to itself (its own ads-on vs. ads-off days), with each calendar day's market-wide baseline estimated and subtracted out
  • Two complementary models: a binary on/off comparison for whether active placements mattered, and a log-log impression-elasticity model for whether scaling volume mattered — with diminishing returns built into the functional form
  • Effects were estimated overall and by customer tier, since a pooled average could hide who actually benefited

Execution

  • Daily dispensary-level panel of sales and ads activity; standard errors clustered by dispensary; tier effects via interaction terms
  • Robustness through re-specification: first-differences models at both daily and weekly grain independently reproduced a significant effect in the same range, so the finding wasn't an artifact of one modeling choice
  • Causality bounded honestly: the design removed fixed dispensary traits and market-wide shocks, but couldn't rule out simultaneous changes (a promo launched alongside ads) — flagged explicitly, with a user-level A/B test named as the definitive follow-up

Outcome

  • Dispensaries saw significantly more GMV and more orders on days sponsored placements were active, with essentially no change in basket size — ads converted more browsers into buyers rather than making buyers spend more
  • More impressions drove more revenue with diminishing returns, and the effect was significant in every tier — including the smallest dispensaries, where the blunt on/off signal alone was too noisy to detect
  • Larger dispensaries showed the smallest incremental returns to scaling impressions, consistent with a higher baseline that was harder to move
 dbt Optimization

$50k Annual Compute Savings

Problem

  • Nightly dbt runtime had more than doubled over the course of a year, quietly eating into the morning reporting window
  • Runs were sometimes still finishing when East Coast employees logged on, so they started their day looking at stale data

Approach

  • Identified the long-running models actually driving the runtime growth
  • Triaged each one: optimize it for real usage, or deprecate it if it wasn't earning its runtime

Execution

  • Queried usage logs to find models that were active in the DAG but never actually surfaced to stakeholders in the BI layer, and removed them
  • Materialized the largest remaining models as incremental instead of full-refresh
  • Rebuilt remaining models around actual use cases and their primary stakeholders
  • Right-sized warehouse compute for the models that still needed it

Outcome

  • Cut nightly runtime by 40%
  • Cut compute costs by $50k annually
  • Established more rigorous modeling guidelines to keep the DAG from bloating again
 Automation

Support Agent Scorecard

Problem

  • Support needed one unified view of agent performance, but the team worked across several different platforms to manage customer communication and workload
  • Manual reporting existed, but even with templates it took several people and several hours a month to put together — and still had errors

Approach

  • Found a join key that actually held across every system, rather than forcing a data model onto systems that were never built to talk to each other
  • Got buy-in on metric definitions before building anything, so the numbers wouldn't get re-litigated after the fact

Execution

  • Agent email turned out to be the only identifier consistent across every system, so everything anchored on that
  • Built an agent-date scaffold with metrics pre-aggregated daily for speed, while keeping the underlying marts detailed enough to drop into a specific ticket, call, or chat when someone needed to check the receipts

Outcome

  • Eliminated 55+ hours of manual reporting a month
  • More trustworthy numbers than the manual process ever produced
  • Went beyond what Zendesk's own reporting could do, with much tighter metric definitions to match
